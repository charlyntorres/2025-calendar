Bonifacio Day: Honoring the Father of the Philippine Revolution

Every November 30, the Philippines celebrates Bonifacio Day, a national holiday dedicated to the life and legacy of Andrés Bonifacio, one of the most prominent heroes of the Philippine Revolution against Spanish colonial rule. Known as the "Father of the Philippine Revolution," Bonifacio's courage and leadership continue to inspire generations of Filipinos to fight for freedom and justice.


The Life of Andrés Bonifacio

Early Life
  • Born on November 30, 1863, in Tondo, Manila, Andrés Bonifacio grew up in a humble household. Despite limited formal education, he was self-taught, developing a love for reading revolutionary works, including Rizal's novels and the writings of international thinkers like Victor Hugo and José Martí.
Founding the Katipunan
  • In 1892, Bonifacio co-founded the Kataas-taasan, Kagalang-galangang Katipunan ng mga Anak ng Bayan (KKK) or the Katipunan, a secret society that sought independence from Spanish rule through revolution.
  • Known for his fiery patriotism, Bonifacio galvanized the masses, spreading the revolutionary cause to workers, farmers, and ordinary Filipinos.
The Cry of Pugad Lawin
  • On August 23, 1896, Bonifacio and his followers tore their cedulas (residence certificates) in a symbolic act of defiance against Spanish oppression, marking the start of the Philippine Revolution.
Leadership and Legacy
  • Despite internal conflicts within the revolution, Bonifacio remains a symbol of grassroots resistance and the fight for equality. His tragic death in 1897 at the hands of fellow revolutionaries solidified his status as a martyr for the cause of freedom.

The Significance of November 30

  • Bonifacio Day has been observed since 1921, when the Philippine Legislature passed Act No. 2946, designating his birth anniversary as a national holiday.
  • The date commemorates not only Bonifacio's birth but also his enduring contributions to the struggle for Philippine independence.

Interesting Facts About Andrés Bonifacio

  • The Unlikely Leader: Unlike most Filipino heroes who came from the elite class, Bonifacio was of humble origins, working as a warehouse clerk and selling canes and paper fans to support his family.
  • The Bonifacio Monument: The Bonifacio Monument in Caloocan City is one of the most recognizable landmarks in the Philippines. Unveiled in 1933, it symbolizes Bonifacio's pivotal role in the revolution.
  • Writer and Orator: Bonifacio was a gifted writer and orator. His poem "Pag-ibig sa Tinubuang Lupa" is a heartfelt ode to love for one's country.
  • The Supremo: Bonifacio was called “Supremo”, or supreme leader, by members of the Katipunan due to his leadership and dedication to the revolutionary cause.
  • Controversial Death: Bonifacio's death remains one of the most debated events in Philippine history, as he was executed by fellow revolutionaries loyal to Emilio Aguinaldo during a power struggle.

Bonifacio's Legacy in Modern Times

Andrés Bonifacio remains a powerful symbol of the Filipino spirit: resilient, brave, and unyielding in the face of oppression. His life teaches the value of collective action and the importance of fighting for justice, no matter how insurmountable the odds may seem.

On Bonifacio Day, Filipinos not only honor a national hero but also renew their commitment to the ideals of freedom, equality, and patriotism. Bonifacio's legacy continues to remind the nation of the sacrifices made by its forebears and the responsibility of every Filipino to uphold the principles of democracy and independence.
