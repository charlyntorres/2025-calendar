The Day of Valor: Honoring Courage and Sacrifice

The Day of Valor, or "Araw ng Kagitingan", is a national holiday in the Philippines observed every April 9th. It commemorates the bravery and resilience of Filipino and American soldiers during World War II, particularly in the face of the infamous Bataan Death March. This day serves as a poignant reminder of the sacrifices made in the fight for freedom and independence.


The History of the Day of Valor

The Day of Valor traces its roots to April 9, 1942, when Filipino and American forces surrendered to Japanese troops after months of fierce fighting in the Bataan Peninsula. With supplies depleted and reinforcements unavailable, over 76,000 soldiers were forced to endure the brutal Bataan Death March.

  • The Bataan Death March:

  • The surrender marked the beginning of a harrowing journey for the captured soldiers, who were forced to march approximately 96 kilometers from Mariveles, Bataan, to San Fernando, Pampanga, under grueling conditions.

    • Thousands died from starvation, dehydration, exhaustion, and abuse along the way.
    • Survivors faced even greater hardships in prison camps.

Despite the suffering, their courage and sacrifice became a symbol of unwavering resilience and patriotism.


Why April 9th?

April 9th was chosen to honor the surrender and subsequent heroism of the defenders of Bataan. Over time, this date evolved into a day of reflection and pride for the Filipino spirit, recognizing not only the struggles of the past but also the enduring quest for freedom.


Modern Observances of the Day of Valor

The Day of Valor is a public holiday, observed with ceremonies and tributes across the Philippines. Key activities include:

  • Commemorative Programs: Events are held at historical sites such as the Mount Samat National Shrine in Bataan, also known as the Dambana ng Kagitingan. Government officials, military personnel, and veterans pay homage to those who fought in World War II.
  • Wreath-Laying Ceremonies: Floral offerings are placed at memorials to honor the fallen heroes.
  • Educational Activities: Schools and institutions host programs to teach younger generations about the significance of the holiday.

Interesting Facts About the Day of Valor

  • Mount Samat National Shrine: Built in 1970, the shrine includes a towering memorial cross standing 92 meters tall, symbolizing the bravery of the Bataan defenders.
  • A Shared History with the United States: The Day of Valor commemorates the shared struggle of Filipino and American soldiers during World War II, highlighting the alliance forged in battle.
  • Name Change: Originally called Bataan Day, the holiday was renamed the Day of Valor to encompass the broader heroism of Filipino fighters beyond Bataan.
  • Honoring Civilians: While the day focuses on soldiers, it also acknowledges the contributions of civilians who aided in the resistance or suffered under occupation.
